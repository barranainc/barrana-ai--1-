# Catering Company Automation: Enquiries, Quoting, Dietary Requirements, Production, and Delivery

**Catering automation connects enquiry response, quoting, dietary and allergen capture, production planning, staff scheduling, and delivery coordination into one workflow.** An enquiry arrives at 11pm and receives an immediate response with menu information and a consultation link. Confirmed events collect guest counts and dietary requirements on a schedule rather than through a dozen emails. Production lists, purchase orders, and staff assignments generate from the actual booked events instead of being rebuilt every week.

For an operator running 5 to 100 events per month, this typically recovers 20 to 40 hours per week and sharply reduces the errors that damage a catering reputation.

---

## Catering Runs on Information That Arrives Late

Catering has a structural timing problem that no other food business shares.

You are quoting an event that happens in three months, for a guest count that will change four times, with dietary requirements that arrive the week before, in a venue you may not have seen, staffed by people you will schedule two weeks out.

Every one of those variables moves, and each movement cascades. A guest count changing from 120 to 145 changes the food order, the equipment, the staffing, and the price. When that information lives in an email thread, the cascade happens manually and something gets missed.

The second problem is that catering errors are highly visible. A restaurant that gets an order wrong serves one table. A caterer who gets an order wrong ruins an event that someone has been planning for a year, in front of two hundred people, with no opportunity to fix it.

And the highest-stakes version of that error is dietary. Serving a guest something they told you they could not eat is, depending on the reason, either a serious service failure or a medical emergency.

### Where caterers lose money and reputation

**Slow enquiry response.** Event planning happens in bursts. A couple or an event planner contacting five caterers will shortlist whoever replied first.

**Quoting time.** Building custom quotes manually takes hours per enquiry, most of which do not convert.

**Guest count churn.** Final numbers arriving late or changing after ordering means either waste or shortage.

**Dietary requirement gaps.** Requirements collected inconsistently, communicated verbally to the kitchen, and not tracked to the plate.

**Production planning by hand.** Rebuilding prep lists, purchase orders, and equipment lists weekly from event details.

**Staffing chaos.** Event staff scheduled by phone, with last-minute gaps handled by scrambling.

## Allergens and Dietary Requirements: The Part That Must Not Fail

This section comes before the workflows because it constrains all of them.

Food allergies can be fatal. Anaphylaxis from an undisclosed allergen at a catered event is a genuine risk, not a theoretical one, and it has happened to caterers who were otherwise competent.

Automation helps here - structured capture is far more reliable than a note on an order form - but only if it is designed with the failure modes in mind.

**Design principles that matter:**

- **Capture structurally, never as free text alone.** A dropdown for common allergens plus a free-text field for detail. Free text alone means "no nuts" and "nut allergy" and "allergic to almonds" are three different strings that a system cannot act on.
- **Distinguish severity.** A preference, an intolerance, and a life-threatening allergy require different handling. Collect that distinction explicitly.
- **Distinguish religious and dietary requirements from allergens.** Halal, kosher, vegetarian, and vegan requirements are about what is acceptable to eat. Allergens are about what is safe. They need different handling in the kitchen and should not be collapsed into one field.
- **Carry the requirement to the plate.** The requirement must appear on the production list, the plating instruction, and the service label - not just on the order.
- **Never let automation confirm safety.** A system can record that a dish contains no listed allergen ingredients. It cannot verify cross-contamination, supplier substitutions, or what actually happened in the kitchen. Only a person can, and only through actual controls.
- **Escalate uncertainty to a human.** If a requirement is unclear or unusual, it must reach the chef, not be resolved by the system.

**Regulatory context:** food businesses in Ontario operate under the Food Premises regulation and municipal public health inspection, with food handler training requirements. Federal labelling rules under the Food and Drugs Act govern priority allergen declaration on packaged foods. Catering has specific obligations around transport temperature control and service. Confirm your obligations with your local public health unit - requirements differ by municipality and by what you serve.

Have your food safety lead review any workflow touching allergens before it goes live.

## The Four Workflows That Matter Most

### Workflow 1: Enquiry response, quoting, and booking

**What happens now:** Enquiries arrive by phone, email, and social. Quotes are built individually, often at night.

**What automation changes:**

1. All enquiry channels feed one queue with immediate acknowledgment at any hour.
2. Structured intake captures event date, type, guest count estimate, venue, service style, budget range, and any known dietary considerations.
3. Date availability checks against booked capacity, so an unavailable date gets an honest immediate answer.
4. Menu packages with per-head pricing generate a preliminary quote within minutes for standard event types.
5. Custom and complex events route to a consultation booking rather than an automated quote.
6. Quotes deliver with clear inclusions, and accepted quotes collect deposit and contract signature in one flow.
7. Unresponded quotes follow up at day 3, 7, and 14.
8. Enquiry source and conversion are tracked.

**Operational impact:** Response time drops from days to minutes. Quote turnaround for standard events drops from hours to minutes, and conversion typically improves 25 to 40 percent.

### Workflow 2: Event detail collection and dietary capture

**What happens now:** Details arrive through email over months. Final numbers and dietary requirements arrive late, sometimes days before.

**What automation changes:**

1. A structured collection sequence runs across the lead time rather than compressing into the final week.
2. Guest count checkpoints are scheduled with clear deadlines tied to ordering lead times.
3. Dietary requirements are collected through a structured form - allergens by category with severity, religious requirements, and preferences captured separately.
4. Where the client is coordinating guest responses, they receive a shareable collection link so guests report their own requirements rather than the client transcribing them.
5. Unusual or unclear requirements flag for the chef to review directly with the client.
6. Venue details - access, kitchen facilities, power, service areas, load-in restrictions - are collected on a schedule.
7. Timeline and service sequence are confirmed and distributed.
8. Changes after ordering deadlines flag with cost implications stated rather than absorbed silently.
9. A final event brief consolidates everything and goes to the client for confirmation.

**Operational impact:** Dietary requirements received before the ordering deadline typically move from 50–70 percent to over 90 percent. Late guest count changes causing waste or shortage drop substantially.

### Workflow 3: Production planning and purchasing

**What happens now:** Prep lists and purchase orders are built weekly from event sheets, by hand.

**What automation changes:**

1. Production requirements calculate from booked events, menus, and confirmed guest counts.
2. Recipe scaling generates ingredient quantities without manual arithmetic.
3. Purchase orders aggregate across events by supplier and generate on each supplier's ordering deadline.
4. Prep schedules distribute across the days before each event, accounting for shelf life and kitchen capacity.
5. Dietary and allergen requirements appear on production lists, prep instructions, and plating specifications - carried through rather than noted once.
6. Special dietary items are flagged for separate preparation and labelling.
7. Equipment requirements per event are listed and checked against availability.
8. Received goods are checked against orders, with shortages flagged early enough to substitute.
9. Food cost per event calculates from actual purchasing, which most caterers cannot see clearly.

**Operational impact:** Production planning time typically drops 60 to 75 percent. Food cost visibility per event frequently reveals that certain event types or menu items are far less profitable than assumed.

### Workflow 4: Staffing, delivery, and post-event

**What happens now:** Staff are scheduled by phone and text. Delivery logistics are worked out per event. Follow-up is inconsistent.

**What automation changes:**

1. Staffing requirements calculate from event size, service style, and venue.
2. Available staff receive shift offers and confirm directly, rather than being phoned individually.
3. Unfilled shifts escalate ahead of the event rather than on the morning.
4. Staff receive event briefings: venue address, load-in details, uniform, timeline, service sequence, and any dietary handling requirements.
5. Delivery and load-out schedules are built with equipment lists and vehicle assignments.
6. Temperature records during transport are captured where required.
7. Post-event, equipment returns are reconciled and losses identified.
8. Final invoicing generates from the actual event including any additions.
9. Feedback and review requests go out at the right moment, and testimonials are requested with clear consent.

**Operational impact:** Staff scheduling time typically drops 60 to 80 percent. Equipment loss drops through systematic reconciliation. Post-event revenue - repeat bookings and referrals - improves through consistent follow-up.

## What Should Stay Human

**Keep human:** menu design and creative decisions, any dietary requirement that is unclear or unusual, final verification of allergen handling, tasting consultations, pricing on complex events, handling a problem during service, and every decision about whether a dish is safe for a specific guest.

**Automate:** enquiry acknowledgment, standard quoting, detail collection scheduling, requirement capture structure, production calculation, purchase order generation, staff shift offering, briefing distribution, invoicing, and follow-up.

The kitchen's judgment is never automated. The system's job is to make sure the kitchen has complete, structured, timely information - and to make it impossible for a dietary requirement to reach service without having been seen by someone.

## Common Questions

**Can automation guarantee an allergen-free dish?**
No, and any system claiming to should be rejected. Automation ensures the requirement is captured, carried to production, and visible at plating. Whether the dish is actually safe depends on ingredient verification, cross-contamination controls, and kitchen discipline - all of which are human and physical, not digital.

**We serve halal, and some clients need certification assurance.**
Certification status of suppliers and ingredients should be tracked at the product level so it is verifiable rather than assumed. Where a client requires certified halal, that becomes a documented requirement carried through purchasing and production, not a verbal understanding.

**Our clients change guest counts constantly.**
That will not stop, but structured checkpoints with clear deadlines and stated cost implications change the dynamic considerably. Most late changes happen because nobody set a deadline the client took seriously.

**How do we handle guests reporting their own dietary requirements?**
A shareable collection link is usually better than the client transcribing - it reduces transcription errors and gets more detail. Make sure severity is captured, not just the allergen.

**What about temperature records during transport?**
Capture them digitally against the event record. If you are ever questioned about a foodborne illness complaint, having contemporaneous records rather than reconstructed ones matters enormously.

**What is the smallest useful starting point?**
Structured dietary requirement capture with carry-through to production. It is the highest-consequence workflow in the business, and it is the one most caterers are handling with the least rigour.
