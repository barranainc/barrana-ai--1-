# Custom Bakery Automation: Order Intake, Design Approval, Production Scheduling, and Delivery

**Custom bakery automation connects order enquiry, design consultation, deposit collection, production scheduling, allergen handling, and delivery coordination into one workflow.** An enquiry arrives with a date and a design idea and receives an immediate response with availability and pricing guidance. Confirmed orders move through structured design approval. Production schedules build backward from delivery dates automatically, so nothing is discovered late on a Friday.

For a bakery producing 10 to 200 custom orders per month, this typically recovers 12 to 25 hours per week and eliminates the scheduling errors that are catastrophic in a date-locked business.

---

## Every Order Has a Hard Deadline

Custom bakery work has an unforgiving structure: the delivery date cannot move.

A wedding is on Saturday. A birthday is on the 14th. A corporate event is at 2pm Thursday. There is no version of this business where you deliver late and it is fine.

That means capacity planning is the entire operation. You need to know, at the moment you accept an order, whether you can actually produce it alongside everything else already booked for that week - accounting for decorating time, oven capacity, fridge space, and the fact that three-tier wedding cakes and forty dozen cupcakes compete for the same hands.

Most small bakeries manage this with a calendar and the owner's memory. That works until it does not, and when it fails it fails publicly.

The second problem is design communication. Custom cake work is visual and subjective. A client describes what they want, the baker interprets it, and the gap between those two things surfaces on delivery day when it is too late to fix.

### Where custom bakeries lose money

**Enquiry response delay.** Cake enquiries cluster in evenings and weekends when the baker is producing or resting.

**Quoting time.** Custom pricing requires assessing design complexity, and doing it individually for every enquiry consumes hours.

**Overbooking.** Accepting orders without real capacity visibility, then working all night to deliver.

**Design misalignment.** No structured approval means expectation gaps discovered at delivery.

**Deposit and payment friction.** Chasing deposits and final payments manually.

**Allergen risk.** Custom orders frequently involve dietary requirements, and informal handling is dangerous.

## Allergen and Dietary Handling

Bakery work involves the most common serious allergens - wheat, eggs, dairy, nuts, soy - in a production environment where cross-contamination is genuinely difficult to control.

Structured capture and carry-through matter, with the same principles that apply across food businesses:

- Capture allergens structurally with severity, not as free text alone.
- Distinguish allergy from preference from religious requirement.
- Carry the requirement to the production sheet and the label.
- Be honest about cross-contamination. A shared kitchen producing wheat products cannot credibly claim gluten-free. Automation should surface that limitation to the client at order time rather than allowing an order you cannot safely fulfil.
- Never let a system confirm safety. A person verifies ingredients and controls.

**Labelling matters here specifically.** Federal requirements under the Food and Drugs Act govern priority allergen declaration on packaged foods, and municipal public health requirements apply to food premises in Ontario. What must appear on a label depends on how the product is sold. Confirm your obligations with your local public health unit and, where you sell packaged goods, with someone familiar with federal labelling rules.

## The Four Workflows That Matter Most

### Workflow 1: Enquiry, quoting, and booking

**What happens now:** Enquiries arrive by Instagram, phone, email, and website form. The baker replies between production tasks.

**What automation changes:**

1. All channels feed one queue with immediate acknowledgment.
2. Structured intake captures date, occasion, servings needed, design ideas or reference images, flavour preferences, dietary requirements, and delivery or pickup.
3. Date capacity is checked in real time, so unavailable dates get an honest immediate answer rather than a maybe.
4. Standard products - cupcakes, sheet cakes, common tiered configurations - receive automated pricing immediately.
5. Complex custom work routes to a consultation with the design brief already captured.
6. Quotes deliver with clear inclusions and a deposit link.
7. Deposits are collected at booking, which is what converts an enquiry into a held date.
8. Unresponded quotes follow up at day 3 and day 7.
9. Enquiries for booked-out dates are offered nearby dates or referred, rather than simply declined.

**Operational impact:** Response time drops from days to minutes. Booking conversion typically improves 25 to 40 percent, and the deposit-at-booking change alone eliminates most of the phantom bookings that block capacity without producing revenue.

### Workflow 2: Design approval and specification

**What happens now:** Design is discussed verbally or through message threads. The baker builds from their interpretation.

**What automation changes:**

1. Design references and inspiration images are collected through the intake rather than scattered across a message thread.
2. A written specification - tiers, sizes, flavours, fillings, colours, finish, decorative elements, inscription text - is confirmed with the client.
3. Inscription text is confirmed in writing and shown back for approval, because misspelled names are among the most common and most avoidable failures.
4. Where the bakery provides sketches or mockups, approval is captured explicitly with a record.
5. Changes after approval flag with cost and timeline implications rather than being absorbed.
6. Change deadlines are stated and enforced, tied to production lead times.
7. The approved specification becomes the production sheet, so what the kitchen builds is what the client approved.
8. Allergen and dietary requirements are locked into the specification.

**Operational impact:** Design disputes and remakes drop substantially. The inscription confirmation alone eliminates a recurring, entirely preventable failure.

### Workflow 3: Production scheduling and capacity

**What happens now:** The baker holds the week in their head and a calendar. Capacity limits are discovered by working until 3am.

**What automation changes:**

1. Each order carries an estimated production time by component - baking, filling, covering, decorating.
2. Production schedules build backward from delivery dates, accounting for what must be done fresh and what can be prepared ahead.
3. Capacity is visible per day and per week, so booking decisions are informed rather than optimistic.
4. Oven, fridge, and workspace constraints are factored, since these are the real limits in a small bakery.
5. Ingredient requirements aggregate across the week into purchase orders.
6. Prep tasks distribute across available days rather than clustering.
7. Orders at risk of a capacity conflict flag early, when the schedule can still be adjusted.
8. Staff scheduling aligns to production load.
9. Special dietary orders are scheduled with separate preparation and equipment where required.

**Operational impact:** Overbooking incidents typically drop 70 to 90 percent. The visibility change is the point - a baker who can see that the third weekend in June is full can decline or reschedule rather than accepting and suffering.

### Workflow 4: Delivery, payment, and follow-up

**What happens now:** Delivery is arranged per order. Final payments are chased. Contact ends at delivery.

**What automation changes:**

1. Delivery schedules build from the day's orders with routing and timing.
2. Clients receive delivery confirmation with a window, and a notification when the driver is en route.
3. Setup requirements at venues - table, stand, timing, contact person - are captured ahead and given to the driver.
4. Delivery confirmation captures a photo, which protects against later damage disputes.
5. Final payment is collected before delivery, automatically reminded rather than chased.
6. Post-delivery, review and photo-sharing requests go out at the right moment.
7. Client photos, with consent, become portfolio content.
8. Anniversary and repeat-occasion reminders reach past clients with consent, which is a meaningful repeat revenue source.
9. Wedding clients are worth particular follow-up, since they generate referrals into a tightly connected market.

**Operational impact:** Payment chasing largely disappears. Repeat and referral business typically improves substantially through consistent follow-up that previously did not happen.

## What Should Stay Human

**Keep human:** design consultation and creative direction, pricing judgment on complex work, any dietary requirement that is unclear, verification of allergen handling and cross-contamination limits, decisions about whether an order is producible, and conversations with a disappointed client.

**Automate:** enquiry acknowledgment, standard quoting, capacity checking, specification recording, approval capture, production scheduling, ingredient aggregation, delivery coordination, payment collection, and follow-up.

## Common Questions

**Can we accept gluten-free orders from a shared kitchen?**
Only with honest disclosure. A kitchen producing wheat products cannot guarantee gluten-free, and clients with celiac disease need to know that clearly before ordering. Build the disclosure into the order flow rather than hoping it comes up in conversation.

**Most of our enquiries come through Instagram DMs.**
Those can feed the same intake. The automation acknowledges immediately and moves the conversation to a structured intake form, which is far better than trying to capture design details in a DM thread.

**How accurate can automated pricing be for custom work?**
Reliable for standard configurations and sizes. Genuinely custom work should route to a human quote, with the automation having already captured the brief so the baker is not starting from nothing.

**We are a two-person operation. Is capacity tracking worth it?**
Particularly so. Small bakeries overbook because capacity lives in one person's head, and the cost is unpaid overnight labour. Making it visible is the whole benefit.

**What about seasonal peaks?**
Capacity visibility matters most at peaks. Knowing in October that the second weekend of December is full lets you either stop selling or hire - both better than discovering it on December 10th.

**What is the smallest useful starting point?**
Enquiry intake with capacity checking and deposit collection. It stops overbooking, converts enquiries faster, and eliminates held dates that never become paying orders.
