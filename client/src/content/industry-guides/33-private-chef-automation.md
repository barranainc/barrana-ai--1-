# Private Chef Automation: Enquiries, Menu Planning, Client Preferences, and Event Execution

**Private chef automation handles enquiry response, menu proposal and approval, dietary and preference capture, shopping and prep planning, and repeat client management so the chef spends their time cooking rather than coordinating.** An enquiry arrives with a date and a party size and receives an immediate response with availability and pricing structure. Confirmed bookings collect dietary requirements, kitchen details, and service preferences through a structured sequence. Shopping lists and prep timelines generate from the approved menu.

For a chef running 4 to 40 events per month, this typically recovers 8 to 18 hours per week and turns one-time bookings into repeat clients.

---

## A One-Person Business With Three Jobs

A private chef is simultaneously running sales, operations, and production, usually alone.

Sales means responding to enquiries fast enough to win them, proposing menus, and closing. Operations means collecting dietary requirements, understanding an unfamiliar kitchen, planning shopping, and coordinating timing. Production is the actual cooking, which is the only part that is why anyone became a chef.

The ratio is unfavourable. For a four-hour dinner service, a chef routinely spends six to ten hours on enquiry handling, menu development, shopping planning, and communication. That unpaid time caps how many events are possible in a week and makes the effective hourly rate far lower than the headline price suggests.

The second problem is that private chef work should be a repeat business and usually is not. A client who has a good experience will book again - for another dinner party, a holiday, a family occasion. But most chefs have no mechanism for staying in contact, so each booking starts from cold.

### Where private chefs lose money

**Slow enquiry response.** Private dining enquiries are often impulsive and comparison-shopped.

**Menu proposal time.** Building a bespoke proposal for every enquiry, most of which do not convert.

**Incomplete dietary information.** Discovered on arrival, when substitution options are limited.

**Kitchen surprises.** Arriving to find no oven space, insufficient burners, or missing equipment.

**Shopping inefficiency.** Building lists from scratch and shopping without a consolidated plan.

**No repeat mechanism.** Every booking acquired fresh.

## Dietary Requirements in a Client's Home

Private chef work has a specific allergen risk profile worth naming.

You are cooking in an unfamiliar kitchen, for guests you have not met, often with information relayed second-hand by the host. The host may not know that their guest's shellfish allergy is anaphylactic rather than a preference. Cross-contamination controls in a domestic kitchen are whatever you bring with you.

Practical principles:

- Capture requirements structurally with severity, and push for direct information rather than accepting "one of them doesn't eat dairy."
- Where a serious allergy is present, confirm the specifics - including whether trace exposure is a risk - before finalizing the menu.
- Distinguish allergy from religious requirement from preference, since each is handled differently in the kitchen.
- Know what you can and cannot control in a domestic kitchen, and decline rather than accept a booking where you cannot safely accommodate a severe allergy.
- Never let a system confirm safety. Structured capture reduces the chance of a requirement being missed; it does not verify anything.

Ontario food premises requirements and municipal public health rules may apply to your operation depending on how you work - whether you prepare in a licensed kitchen, cook entirely on site, or transport prepared food. Confirm with your local public health unit, since the answer varies by municipality and by your specific model.

## The Four Workflows That Matter Most

### Workflow 1: Enquiry response and booking

**What happens now:** Enquiries arrive by Instagram, referral, website, and platform listings. The chef replies when not cooking.

**What automation changes:**

1. All channels feed one queue with immediate acknowledgment.
2. Structured intake captures date, time, guest count, occasion, location, service style, budget range, and known dietary requirements.
3. Availability is checked against the calendar, so unavailable dates get an immediate honest answer.
4. Pricing structure - per head ranges by service style, plus travel and shopping - is communicated immediately rather than after a conversation.
5. Standard formats receive an indicative quote automatically; bespoke work routes to a consultation.
6. Deposit and agreement are collected in one flow at booking.
7. Unresponded enquiries follow up at day 2 and day 6.
8. Enquiries for booked dates are offered alternatives or referred to trusted colleagues.

**Operational impact:** Response time drops from hours or days to minutes. Booking conversion typically improves 25 to 45 percent, driven by speed and by clear pricing communicated upfront rather than negotiated.

### Workflow 2: Menu proposal and approval

**What happens now:** Menus are written individually for each enquiry, often before a booking is confirmed.

**What automation changes:**

1. Menu proposals draw from the chef's own tested repertoire, filtered by season, service style, dietary constraints, and budget.
2. Proposals generate as a structured document rather than being written from scratch.
3. Clients review and select from options rather than an open-ended conversation.
4. Dietary constraints are applied before the proposal is built, so incompatible dishes never appear.
5. Approved menus lock with a change deadline tied to shopping.
6. Wine or beverage pairing suggestions attach where the chef offers them.
7. Course timing and service sequence are documented and shared.
8. Approved menus become the basis for shopping and prep planning automatically.

**Operational impact:** Menu proposal time typically drops from 1 to 3 hours per enquiry to 15 to 30 minutes. Since most enquiries do not convert, this is where the largest unpaid time recovery happens.

### Workflow 3: Event preparation and logistics

**What happens now:** Kitchen details, dietary confirmations, and timing are worked out through message threads in the days before.

**What automation changes:**

1. A structured pre-event sequence collects what is needed on a schedule.
2. Kitchen details - oven type and capacity, burners, refrigeration, workspace, equipment available - are captured through a checklist so surprises are eliminated.
3. Final guest count confirms by a stated deadline tied to shopping.
4. Dietary requirements confirm per guest, with severity, ahead of the menu locking.
5. Access details - parking, entry, load-in, timing - are collected.
6. Shopping lists generate from the approved menu scaled to final guest count.
7. Prep timelines build backward from service time, distinguishing what can be prepped ahead from what must be done on site.
8. Equipment the chef must bring is listed based on the kitchen checklist responses.
9. A client-facing timeline confirms arrival, service, and departure times.

**Operational impact:** Kitchen surprises on arrival typically drop 70 to 85 percent. Shopping efficiency improves substantially through consolidated lists, and the prep timeline reduces on-site improvisation.

### Workflow 4: Post-event and repeat business

**What happens now:** The event ends. Payment is settled. Contact stops.

**What automation changes:**

1. Final payment is collected automatically per the agreement.
2. A follow-up message goes out shortly after the event, when satisfaction is highest.
3. Review and referral requests follow at the right moment.
4. Client preferences - what they loved, what they did not, their guests' requirements, their kitchen setup - are retained, so a repeat booking starts with everything known.
5. Occasion-based follow-ups reach past clients ahead of holidays, anniversaries, and entertaining seasons, with consent.
6. Clients who booked once but not again enter a low-frequency re-engagement sequence.
7. Referrals from past clients are tracked and acknowledged.
8. Repeat rate and client lifetime value become visible.

**Operational impact:** Repeat booking rates typically improve substantially - often from under 20 percent to 35 to 50 percent - because the mechanism exists where previously there was none. Retained client preferences also make repeat bookings dramatically faster to plan.

## What Should Stay Human

**Keep human:** menu creativity and dish selection, any dietary requirement that is unclear or severe, the decision to decline a booking you cannot safely execute, pricing on unusual events, the client relationship, and everything that happens during service.

**Automate:** enquiry acknowledgment, availability checking, proposal assembly, detail collection scheduling, shopping list generation, prep timeline building, payment collection, follow-up, and preference retention.

The cooking and the hosting are the product. Everything else is overhead that should not be consuming a chef's evenings.

## Common Questions

**My menus are bespoke. Won't templating make them generic?**
The proposal assembles from your own dishes, which are already yours. What is automated is the document building and the filtering by constraints - not the creative decision about what to serve. Most chefs find they have a repertoire of eighty dishes they draw from repeatedly and had been rewriting descriptions for each time.

**How do I handle a host who does not know their guests' allergies precisely?**
Push for direct information. Offer a link the host can share with guests so requirements come from the person who has them. Where a severe allergy is involved and the details are vague, that is a conversation, not a form.

**Can I decline a booking over allergen risk?**
Yes, and sometimes you should. A domestic kitchen with no ability to control cross-contamination is not a safe environment for a guest with anaphylactic allergies to certain ingredients. Declining honestly is better than accepting and hoping.

**What about platform bookings from marketplace sites?**
Those can feed the same intake. The value is that your own repeat business grows outside the platform, which is where the margin is.

**Do clients mind structured forms for something this personal?**
Generally not, provided the tone matches your brand and the personal contact is still there. Clients appreciate not having to remember to tell you about the kitchen or the guest count.

**What is the smallest useful starting point?**
Menu proposal assembly. It recovers the largest block of unpaid time, and it applies to every enquiry whether or not it converts.
